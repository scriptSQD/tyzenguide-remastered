Our inspirer - who is Antoni Tyzenhaus?

by Tyzenguide Team

When centuries keep traditions about a certain person, we can say that this person was ahead of his time. Everyone in Hrodna knows about Antoni Tyzenhaus.

Antoni Tyzenhaus is an amazing and in many ways mysterious person, a dreamer, a romantic, a talented organizer.
He was one of the few representatives of power in the history of the Commonwealth, who vividly believed that it was possible to raise production in his state to the level of the best European models. At the same time, he not only believed, but also strove to implement it with all his abilities. It was the patron of production. Manufactures, which he managed to build and run, had a noticeable impact on the development of industry in the Commonwealth, despite the fact that they did not last long.
Count Antoni Tyzenhaus, the son of the Shmeltinsky elder, was born in 1733. He received his initial education at home, then he was assigned to the monastery school of the Jesuits in Vilna.
In 1761 Antoni Tyzenhaus was elected to the Warsaw Seim, and in 1763 he was appointed a great Lithuanian clerk. In 1765 King Stanislav Augustus gave him the post of headman of Hrodna and a Lithuanian outward treasurer. Taking up the matter, Antoni Tyzenhaus set himself a goal worthy of a real altruist: to raise the welfare of his native land. The income of the Hrodna economy, which consisted of 200 villages, went to the royal table. The economy was in complete stagnation. A lot of things had to be corrected… It was necessary to improve crafts, industry, build roads, and build housing. The task was not an easy one. But he was young, educated, and most importantly, his goals coincided with what fate gave him. He felt happy - and this feeling overshadowed any black doubt in him. Having enlisted the support of the king who approved his plans, the treasurer began to act...
He spared neither himself nor those who were under his command. According to eyewitnesses, the treasurer slept no more than three hours a day.
He started with farming. In the very first year he organized a school of land surveyors. On his instructions, they took up the draining of the swamps. Under his personal supervision, the Neman was cleared from Hrodna to Viliya. At the same time, the Mukhovets Canal was built, 8 miles long, connecting the Mukhovets and Pina rivers. At the same time several large bridges and paved roads were built.
There were not enough specialists. And Tyzenhaus took up the organization of education. He founded a medical school and an institute of midwifery in Hrodna. Expensive teachers had to be sent from Paris and Montpellier. One can imagine how difficult it was to revive the work of such institutions. The prejudice against natural knowledge was so great at that time that both institutions did not find those willing to study in them. Tyzenhaus had to make people study. In the first year, the treasurer chose 15 young people from among the serfs. People went to these institutions unwillingly. But by 1755 teaching at the school and institute went with success. This is how the first medical institute in the Grand Duchy began its activity.
In Hrodna Tyzenhaus set up a printing house for "His Royal Majesty" in which a weekly newspaper began to be printed. In the same place several schools were established at once: cadets, accounting, veterinary, medical and surgical. The first public library with an office of natural sciences was founded. The most significant act of the indefatigable treasurer is the construction and operation of many factories by him. Mainly factories were built in Hrodna. Here are those that were launched: gold, silk, cloth, hosiery, linen, linen, lace, hat, carriage, card, pins and needles, weapons, files, steel tools, iron copper products, wax bleaching, paints, leather. In addition, under Tyzenhaus, a cloth factory in Brest and an ironworks in Brest, a linen factory in Shavly were built and began to produce. In Hrodna, under him, the Merchants' Bank was established.
Anthoni Tyzenhaus also opened the first coffee shop in Hrodna in the 1770s. The cafe building was located on the site of today's main building of the Hrodna State University.
The troubles of Antoni Tyzenhaus began in 1780. He did not pay the annual tax levied on factories. It was immediately followed by his removal from the post of treasurer. The case was not brought to court, but it had such an effect on the count that he went crazy - “fell ill with melancholy” ... Without the owner, the factories soon fell into decay.
The main reason for the decline of Tyzenhaus manufactories was the failure to maintain the proportion between production and sale of products. The lack of sales and the small fund of factories led to the fact that the last few months of the year had to stand idle...
The rest of the days Antoni Tyzenhaus lived in seclusion. He died in Warsaw on March 31, 1785 at the age of 52. His body was exhibited in the Carmelite church in the town of Zheludok. There, in the family crypt, the count was buried.

One should speak of Antoni Tyzenhaus only as a national hero. He stood at the origins of the development of the industry of the Grand Duchy. He created and managed a whole network of so-called "royal economies" in the principality, uniting trade, production and storage. The count took on the unbearable burden of the engine of progress.
He wanted to accelerate the movement of the flywheel of history single-handedly- and overstrained.
